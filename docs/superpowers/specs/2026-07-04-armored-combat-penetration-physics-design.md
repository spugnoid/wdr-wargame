# Armored Combat: Penetration Physics Redesign

Status: Draft, pending Rod's review
Scope: Phase 1 of 2 (see "Out of scope" below for phase 2)
Supersedes: parts of Section 17 (Vehicles: Counter Design and Movement) and Section 18 (Vehicle Combat Resolution) as written in docs/source/, specifically the AV/PEN model in 17.2–17.3 and the slope-effect claim in 17.2.6.

## 1. Why this exists

The current AV/PEN system (17.2–18.2) is a reasonable first draft but was built from estimated formulas rather than sourced ballistics data. Two reference documents now sit in the project root — `ww2pen3.pdf` (John D. Salt's cross-checked compilation of ~50 published penetration sources, plus British WO facing/hit-probability data) and `WWII Ballistics- Armor and Gunnery.pdf` (Bird & Livingston, 2nd ed. — the DeMarre equation, slope-multiplier formulas by projectile nose shape, cast/face-hardened/high-hardness/flaw multipliers, shatter gap, mantlet thickness maps, compound-angle math). Both have been fully read and their formulas extracted and spot-validated against a real gun (German 75mm KwK40/PzGr39) with sub-2% error against attested historical penetration figures.

The goal, per Rod's stated design principle: model ammunition nature, slope, angle, incline, compound angle, and armor type with full historical rigor **at design time**, and change nothing about what a player does at the table beyond recalibrating the numbers already printed. No new arithmetic, no new lookups, no new rolls.

## 2. Decisions locked this session

1. **Scope order:** penetration physics (this document) before the hit-location/crew-position/component-damage layer (phase 2, separate design pass).
2. **Ammo selection:** freely chosen by the firer at the moment of firing. A gun's standard-issue round is always available. Scarce/premium rounds (APCR, APDS, HVAP — tungsten-cored) use the existing AMO + secret-bonus mechanic already built for mortars (16.3) and snipers (21.1.3): base AMO printed on the counter, a secret 1d6-1 bonus rolled at setup, an extended-ammunition roll once the base is exhausted. No new resource system.
3. **Ammo granularity:** a gun's counter carries exactly as many PEN lines as ammo natures it actually carried historically — typically two (standard + premium), occasionally three (+ HEAT, for short-barreled/infantry-support guns that carried it). Not padded for completeness, not collapsed for simplicity.
4. **Hull/turret split:** AV becomes two profiles (Hull, Turret) instead of one blended composite (current 17.2.4's 60/40 or 70/30 area weighting is replaced by real data — see §4).
5. **Arcs:** each profile keeps the existing three arcs (Front/Side/Rear). Continuous per-shot angle was considered and explicitly rejected in favor of keeping the arcs and recalibrating them — see §5 for why.
6. **Two AV families per arc per profile**, not one: **AV-vs-Capped** (serves AP/APC/APCBC/APBC kinetic rounds) and **AV-vs-Tungsten** (serves APCR/APDS/HVAP), because these follow structurally different slope-effect curve families, not just different magnitudes of the same curve (confirmed against source data — tungsten's slope multiplier is angle-only, no T/D dependence, and tungsten sometimes performs *worse* against very hard plate due to core shatter). HEAT does not get a printed AV column — see §6.
7. **"Zero lines" at the table**, confirmed as the target: no chart, nomogram, or graphical calculator is used during play. Charts and formulas are design-time/calibration-time tools only. What changes on physical components is: (a) the gun-side PEN-by-range figure becomes a properly curve-fitted stepped value set instead of the current linear `⬡h -f` falloff, and (b) each vehicle prints two AV numbers per arc per profile instead of one.

## 3. Calculation pipeline (design-time only)

### 3.1 Gun-side: PEN(range) per gun+ammo-nature

For each gun+ammo-nature pair:
1. Take the K-factor velocity-at-range formula (Bird & Livingston, Ch.18 p.83): `V(range) = MV × e^(range × 0.7 × -0.0000001 × K)`, using the book's own tabulated K/MV values per projectile where available.
2. Gather attested 0°-or-known-angle penetration figures at 2+ ranges from `ww2pen3.pdf` and/or Bird & Livingston's own penetration tables, converting to 0°-equivalent via the §4 slope formula if the source data isn't already at 0°.
3. Fit `PEN_0deg(V) = P_ref × (V / V_ref)^n` by least-squares across all available calibration points (not a single anchor — the single-anchor approach was tried first and showed ~7% error at range extremes; the least-squares fit across 5 points came in under 1.1% error everywhere tested).
4. Output: a small set of range-band PEN values (0°-equivalent), printed directly on the counter as a stepped ready-reckoner, same "one and done, on the counter" format already agreed for infantry fire lines.

Worked example (75mm KwK40 L/48, PzGr39/APCBC, calibrated against Encyclopedia of German Tanks data): fitted exponent n=1.72 (vs. the generic DeMarre reference of 1.43 — consistent with the source's own point that real exponents drift by projectile), max error 1.1% across 5 calibration ranges (100–2000m). See `/tmp/.../scratchpad/nomo/model.py` for the working implementation.

### 3.2 Target-side: AV per arc per profile

For each vehicle, for each of the 6 arc-profile combinations (Hull F/S/R, Turret F/S/R):
1. Determine the arc's real armor geometry: base thickness, mounting angle range, armor type (RHA/FHA/cast/high-hardness), known flaws (e.g., pre-Oct-1943 Sherman glacis), and — for turret arcs specifically — the actual non-uniform thickness map where documented (Tiger/Panther/Sherman mantlets are not flat plates; Ch.8's thickness maps and Ch.11's turret-hit-probability data should inform an area-weighted average rather than a single nominal figure).
2. Apply the universal slope-multiplier formula for the relevant ammo family (Ch.2 p.20's piecewise F/G equations for capped kinetic rounds; the tungsten and APBC/Soviet-style equivalents still need to be built — see §7) across the arc's representative angle range, combined with the compound-angle formula (`cos(A) = cos(V)·cos(L)`, Ch.12) where hull and turret mounting angles differ from pure vertical slope.
3. Apply cast (Ch.5), face-hardened (Ch.3), high-hardness (Ch.4), and flaw (Ch.6) multipliers as appropriate to that specific plate, using the now-sourced equations rather than the current ruleset's simplified formula.
4. Output: **AV-vs-Capped** and **AV-vs-Tungsten**, one pair per arc per profile (12 numbers total per vehicle, up from the current 3).

This step never happens at the table. It happens once, per vehicle, at design time, exactly like the current 17.2.4 already claims to do — the difference is the formulas behind it are now sourced and validated rather than estimated.

### 3.3 HEAT

HEAT follows a near-pure secant law (`Effective resistance = base thickness / cos(angle)`, Ch.2 p.16) with much weaker sensitivity to cast/face-hardening/flaws than kinetic rounds. Rather than a third printed AV column per vehicle, HEAT should use a **small universal reference table** (angle → secant multiplier) applied to the target's raw base thickness, the same reference reused across every HEAT-armed weapon in the game. ~~Needs a spaced-armor/schürzen interaction rule~~ — **resolved, see §16.** (Correction to an earlier note here: Ch.9's spaced-armor equation does *not* in fact cover HEAT at all — verified directly this session by searching the full chapter text and confirming zero mentions of HEAT/jet/standoff/schürzen anywhere in it. That was an inaccurate inference from an earlier pass, not something the source actually said; §16 explains what the source does and doesn't cover and what §16's rule is based on instead.)

## 4. Existing-rule corrections identified along the way

- **Rule 17.2.6 is factually wrong and should be corrected regardless of how much of this redesign ships:** it currently states "HEAT weapons are immune to slope effect and penetrate regardless." The source data is explicit that HEAT is the *one* ammunition type that reliably follows a clean secant law — it loses real effectiveness at steep angles. What HEAT is actually immune to is **ricochet** (a shaped-charge jet doesn't bounce off a plate at extreme angle the way a kinetic round does), not slope effect generally. Reword to separate these two claims.

## 5. Why arcs were kept over continuous angle

A continuous-angle model was explored and rejected for this pass. The reason: the slope multiplier depends on the *target's* real thickness (the `(T/D)^G` term), not on the gun or the angle alone — so angle can't be resolved into a pure gun-side number the way range can. Fully continuous angle would require a live, per-shot calculation combining exact hex-geometry angle with the target's real armor thickness at that specific point — which is a genuine 2-3 variable per-shot problem, not something that can be pre-baked. Two working prototypes were built to make this concrete (see `/tmp/.../scratchpad/nomo/`): a gun-side range→PEN curve (needs no chart, prints directly on the counter) and a universal target-side angle×T/D→multiplier contour chart (reusable across every gun+target pair for a given ammo family). Rod chose to keep the 3-arc bucketing and use the universal chart purely as a design-time calibration tool, achieving zero chart-reading at the table — the strongest possible reading of "fewest lines."

## 6. Player-facing surface, net result

Nothing changes structurally about how a player resolves vehicle combat (18.1 resolution sequence, 18.2 penetration-outcome bands, 18.3–18.6 damage tables all stay as-is). What changes:
- A vehicle counter prints **two AV numbers per arc** (Capped, Tungsten) instead of one, across **two profiles** (Hull, Turret) instead of one blended composite — 12 numbers instead of 3.
- A gun's PEN line becomes a small set of curve-fitted range-band values instead of a single `rFP ⬡h -f` linear formula (mechanically the same "read a value off your own counter" action as today, just properly calibrated).
- A gun may carry 2-3 PEN lines instead of 1, one per ammo nature it actually carried, freely chosen at fire time; premium ammo tracked via existing AMO/secret-bonus plumbing.
- HEAT hits use a shared reference table (angle → multiplier) applied to a vehicle's raw thickness, not a new AV column.

## 8. Out of scope (phase 2, separate design pass)

Hit-location / crew-position / ammo-and-fuel-location / engine-drivetrain-location / component-damage system remain phase 2. The extracted Shot Placement System (Bird & Livingston Appendix 15) and Theoretical Hit Probability Method (Appendix 6) are the most likely source material and have already been fully extracted; they are not used anywhere in this document. ## 9. Validation approach

Same standard the existing ruleset already holds itself to (18.12): before any number is treated as final, re-run the historical-matchup table (Sherman 75mm vs. Tiger I front/side, Panzerfaust 60 vs. T-34/76, T-34/85 vs. Tiger I, etc.) against the newly-calibrated AV/PEN values and confirm the system result still matches the historically-documented verdict. The gun-side curve-fitting approach (§3.1) already demonstrated this discipline on one worked example (75mm KwK40/PzGr39, <1.2% error across 5 independent historical data points) and should be repeated for every gun+ammo pair before its numbers are treated as final.

## 10. Turret facing and hull/turret hit allocation

Splitting AV into Hull and Turret profiles (§2.4) creates a question the current ruleset never had to answer: given a hit, which profile does it resolve against, and which arc of that profile is presented? Two separate mechanics, decided this session:

**Hull/Turret Hit Allocation** (which structure was hit). First-pass table, extending the shape of the Ch.11 turret-hit-probability data (hits bunch on hull at short range when aim is center-mass, spread toward the turret as range grows, presumably plateauing near the vehicle's real silhouette-area ratio rather than climbing indefinitely — not yet validated beyond the one gun/scenario Ch.11 covered):

> When an attack produces a result of Suppressed or worse against a vehicle with split Hull/Turret AV, roll 1d6:
> — Short range (≤4 hexes tactical / ≤2 hexes operational): 1 = Turret, 2-6 = Hull.
> — Medium/long range (beyond that): 1-2 = Turret, 3-6 = Hull.

Flagged as rough — the Ch.11 data behind it is one gun (76mm APCBC) at one specific analysis, generalized by shape rather than by number. Needs either more source data or a proper per-vehicle silhouette-area calculation before the exact break points are trusted.

**Turret Facing** (which arc of *whichever* structure was hit is presented, when the turret has rotated away from the hull's facing). Modeled directly on the existing Exposed mechanic (6.6) — a state created by a specific declared action, bounded by a specific lifecycle, only relevant if an opponent has a reaction available to exploit it — rather than a new kind of tracked state:

> **TRAVERSED marker.** When a turreted vehicle fires at a target outside its hull's front hexside — i.e., the shot requires the turret to traverse away from the hull's facing arrow, within its TRAV rating — place a TRAVERSED marker on the vehicle, oriented toward the engaged hexside. This shows the turret's actual current facing, independent of the hull's facing arrow.
>
> While a TRAVERSED marker is present, any attack resolving against this vehicle's **turret** (per Hull/Turret Hit Allocation) determines its arc (Front/Side/Rear) relative to the TRAVERSED marker, not the hull's facing arrow. Attacks resolving against the **hull** always use the hull's facing arrow, regardless of the TRAVERSED marker.
>
> The marker is removed the instant the vehicle takes another fire action this turn (replaced by a new one if that shot also requires an off-forward traverse), and in any case is removed at the Recovery Phase along with all other action markers — consistent with the existing rule that the turret returns to forward facing between fire actions (17.4.3).
>
> Casemate vehicles (TRAV 0) never receive a TRAVERSED marker; they have no separate turret profile.

Design rationale: 17.4.3 already establishes that turret facing is inherently temporary (resets between fire actions), so full independent turret-facing tracking was rejected as unnecessary cost — the TRAVERSED marker gets the real tactical behavior (a vehicle that traverses to engage a flank target is genuinely exposed turret-side to a different angle than its hull suggests, for as long as that's actually true) using only information the TRAV system already requires the player to declare, with zero persistent second-facing bookkeeping.

## 11. Shooting/hitting mechanics (new subsystem, this session)

The base game has no hit/miss step at all for vehicle gunnery — every declared shot is assumed to land, and only the penetration outcome is resolved (18.1-18.2). Decided this session: add one, and **unify it with the Hull/Turret Hit Allocation (§10) into a single roll** rather than two separate systems — a large aim-error result is a miss, a small one lands on a specific arc, reusing the same dice-driven logic Appendix 15's Shot Placement System already describes.

**Source data used:** Appendix 6 (Theoretical Hit Probability Method) and Appendix 15 (Shot Placement System), both already extracted before this session; this session added Appendix 7 (Additional Notes on Gun Accuracy — the crew-quality caps, dispersion-quality-by-projectile table, trunnion cant), Appendix 8 (Wind and Drift), Appendix 9 (real Tiger E firing-test data — now used, see below), and Appendix 17 (Accuracy Against Moving Targets — the British O.B. Investigation No.659 study, the core calibration data for the hit-probability model below).

**Deliberately omitted, not modelled:** wind/drift and trunnion cant (Appendix 7/8). Both are first-shot-only effects (corrected on follow-up shots, same as range/lead errors), and the largest wind/drift offset found across every gun in the source's own table was 0.64m at ~1000 yards, rising to 2.2m at ~2000 yards — trivial against any target silhouette at this game's scale. A genuine simplification, not an oversight.

**Base hit-probability model — a real finding, not just a fit.** Appendix 17's own moving-target study gives real first-shot hit% at 5 ranges each for three very different guns (75mm APCBC, 17pdr APCBC, 6pdr APCBC — 15 points total). Plotted against **flight time** rather than raw range, all 15 points collapse onto one clean monotonic curve regardless of which gun produced them — confirming the source's own claim that flight time, not range, is the variable that actually governs hit probability. This means the model generalizes to every gun already in the roster using data already on hand (`muzzle_velocity_fps`/`k_factor`, the same fields calibrated for penetration) — no new per-gun accuracy data needed. Fitted form: `Hit% = 100 × exp(-(flight_time/1.4785)^2.3483)`, mean absolute error 5.7 percentage points across the 15 calibration points (max 12.4pp) — a reasonable fit given the source's own data reflects genuine crew-performance variability, not a clean physical law.

**Crew quality.** Appendix 6/7 caps maximum hit probability by a 6-tier crew-quality label (Elite 90%/Veteran 85%/Experienced 80%/Regular 75%/Green 65%/Militia 50%) the base game doesn't have. Decided this session: derive it from the existing Morale stat rather than adding a new field — Elite=Morale 7+, Veteran=6, Regular=5, Green=3-4, Militia≤2 ("Experienced" folded into Veteran, since Morale is integer-valued and there's no room for a sixth band without new granularity).

**Target-crossing modifier.** Appendix 17 also gives a Direct-Crosser/Head-On hit% ratio by range that is **nearly gun-independent** (checked across all three guns, agreeing within a few percentage points at every range) — collapses into one small universal table, same pattern as the HEAT reference table, rather than a per-gun one. Real numbers: 0.91 at 400yd down to 0.37 at 2000yd for a first shot; 0.99 down to 0.48 for follow-up shots. Worth keeping as a finding in its own right: a crossing target is **never** as easy to hit as a stationary one, even with unlimited ranging shots — the follow-up ratio bottoms out around 0.48-0.53 rather than recovering to 1.0.

**Implemented and tested** (`formulas.py`: `flight_time`, `base_hit_probability`, `crew_quality_hit_cap`/`crew_quality_from_morale`, `crossing_target_ratio`, `hit_probability`; `pipeline.py`: `hit_probability_output.csv` per gun, `gunnery_reference_output.csv` for the two universal tables), 9 new regression tests, all passing.

**Unification — done.** One 1d6+1d8+1d12 roll now resolves Miss/Turret/Hull directly, reusing the exact dice sum the rest of the game already rolls (design note E.7) and its published 576-outcome probability table (Appendix C), rather than inventing a new roll:

> Compute `hit_pct` (gun/range/crew-quality/crossing) and `(turret_frac, hull_frac)` (range-based, §10). Derive two thresholds from the dice's own exact CDF: `miss_threshold` = the roll needed for `P(roll≥T) ≈ hit_pct`; `hull_threshold` = the (higher) roll needed for `P(roll≥T) ≈ hit_pct × hull_frac`. Roll 1d6+1d8+1d12: below `miss_threshold` = Miss; from there up to (not including) `hull_threshold` = Turret; at or above `hull_threshold` = Hull.

The ordering isn't arbitrary — it falls out of the same dispersion logic as everything else here: a shot that barely clears the hit threshold is a marginal/grazing hit, more likely to clip the exposed turret; a shot that clears it comfortably is well-aimed at the bulk of the silhouette, the hull. Verified algebraically (not just by example): reconstructing `P(Turret)` and `P(Hull)` from the two thresholds via the real dice CDF reproduces `hit_pct × turret_frac` and `hit_pct × hull_frac` respectively, which is the actual mathematical claim the unification depends on (see `test_probability_of_each_band_matches_the_component_probabilities`).

Handles the extreme-range edge case correctly rather than breaking: below the dice's resolution floor (rarer than ~0.17%, the probability of rolling the single maximum value 26), `miss_threshold` returns `None` and the shot is an automatic miss — confirmed for Tiger's 88mm at 2500m against a regular crew, where hit% had already rounded to 0.0% in the base curve.

Implemented in `formulas.py` (`roll_threshold_for_probability`, `hull_turret_split`, `VehicleFireThresholds`, `vehicle_fire_thresholds`) and wired into `pipeline.py` (`vehicle_fire_thresholds_output.csv`, per gun/crew-quality/range band). 7 new regression tests, 41 total passing.

**Appendix 9 validation — done.** Appendix 9 (printed p.103-105, "Firing Test Results Against Tiger E") spans three sub-datasets: (1) Jentz's 17pdr APCBC/APDS and 6pdr APCBC test-result table — not independently checkable against this project's own formulas, since no British gun is in the current 13-vehicle roster (no 17pdr/6pdr K-factor or gun curve exists to compare against); flagged as a real coverage gap, not silently skipped. (2) The John Salt Tunisia table and (3) Fletcher's 6pdr-vs-Tiger combat/test table both give the book's own computed 0°-equivalent armor figures for uncapped 6pdr/17pdr AP hits — these directly validated the new `ap_uncapped` ammo family (see §7) to <2% error across 4 points, and separately surfaced the high-hardness-at-moderate-BHN open question also logged in §7. Net result: this validation pass closed the uncapped-AP open item with real cross-checked numbers, and traded it for a narrower, better-understood open question (Tiger-hardness-specific) plus an honestly-flagged coverage gap (no British guns yet).

### HEAT reference table — added this session

`heat_multiplier()` implements the clean secant law from Ch.2 p.16 (no T/D or armor-quality dependence — HEAT jet penetration is far less sensitive to cast/hardness/flaws than kinetic nose-shatter/plugging failure modes). ## 14. Proposed rule text: Gunnery Roll

Drafted in the existing rulebook's voice, ready to slot into Section 18 (exact final numbering TBD at integration time — written here as new Rule 18.1a, with 18.1's existing steps renumbered to make room for it as the new first step). Covers the mechanism designed in §11.

### New definitions (add to 1.3 Definitions and Terminology)

**Gunnery Roll —** The roll made whenever a vehicle-mounted gun fires at another vehicle, resolving in a single roll whether the round hits at all and, if so, whether it strikes the target's Hull or Turret profile. See Rule 18.1a.

**Crew Quality —** A firing vehicle's gunnery skill tier (Elite/Veteran/Regular/Green/Militia), derived from its own Morale value. Caps the maximum hit probability the Gunnery Roll can achieve, representing human factors (stress, fatigue, training) that a purely ballistic model does not capture.

**Miss Threshold —** The lowest 1d6+1d8+1d12 roll that avoids a miss on the Gunnery Roll, printed on the firing vehicle's counter per range band.

**Hull Threshold —** The lowest roll that strikes the Hull profile outright (a roll between the Miss Threshold and the Hull Threshold strikes the Turret instead), printed alongside the Miss Threshold.

**Crossing target —** A target vehicle that moved this turn into a position where the firer's line of fire falls in its Side arc, rather than Front or Rear. Harder to hit than a stationary or directly closing/receding target — see Rule 18.1a.6.

### 18.1 Resolution Sequence Overview (revised)

When a weapon fires at a vehicle, resolve in this order:

**18.1.1** Step 1: Gunnery Roll. Roll to determine whether the round hits the target at all and, if so, whether it strikes the Hull or Turret profile. See Rule 18.1a. **If the result is a Miss, resolution ends here — the round has no further effect.**

**18.1.2** Step 2: Determine arc. Identify whether the attacker is in the target's front, side, or rear arc **of whichever profile (Hull or Turret) the Gunnery Roll selected in Step 1.** Use the corresponding AV value from that profile. *(Existing Rule 18.1.1, renumbered.)*

**18.1.3** Step 3: Check TRAV. Confirm target vehicle's TRAV rating allows it to be engaged this turn. *(Existing 18.1.2.)*

**18.1.4** Step 4: Calculate effective PEN at range using the standard falloff formula. *(Existing 18.1.3.)*

**18.1.5** Step 5: Compare effective PEN to target AV and determine penetration outcome. *(Existing 18.1.4.)*

**18.1.6** Step 6: Apply result using the vehicle damage table. *(Existing 18.1.5.)*

### 18.1a Gunnery Roll

**18.1a.1** Every vehicle-mounted gun counter prints a Gunnery Table: a Miss Threshold and a Hull Threshold for each range band, already calculated for that vehicle's own Crew Quality (Rule 18.1a.2). No separate lookup, chart, or calculation is required at the table — read the row for the actual range.

*Example format (values illustrative — 88mm KwK36, Regular crew):*

.. list-table::
   :header-rows: 1

   * - Range
     - Result
   * - 0–750m
     - Roll < 12: Miss. Roll 12–13: Turret. Roll ≥ 14: Hull.
   * - 1000m
     - Roll < 16: Miss. Roll 16–17: Turret. Roll ≥ 18: Hull.
   * - 1500m
     - Roll < 21: Miss. Roll 21: Turret. Roll ≥ 22: Hull.
   * - 2000m
     - Roll < 25: Miss. Roll 25: Turret. Roll ≥ 26: Hull.
   * - 2500m+
     - Automatic miss. No roll required.

**18.1a.2** Crew Quality is derived from the firing vehicle's own Morale value: Morale 7+ = Elite, Morale 6 = Veteran, Morale 5 = Regular, Morale 3–4 = Green, Morale 2 or less = Militia. This determines which Gunnery Table is printed on the counter — it is fixed at counter-design time, not chosen or looked up during play.

**18.1a.3** Roll 1d6+1d8+1d12 and compare to the Miss Threshold and Hull Threshold for the actual range to target, using the next lower printed range band if the exact range falls between two listed bands.

**18.1a.4** If the roll is below the Miss Threshold, the round misses. No further resolution — the round has struck terrain, passed over the target, or otherwise failed to connect.

**18.1a.5** If the roll meets or exceeds the Hull Threshold, the round strikes the target's Hull profile. If the roll is at or above the Miss Threshold but below the Hull Threshold, the round strikes the Turret profile instead. Proceed to Rule 18.1.2 using the indicated profile.

**18.1a.6** Crossing target: if the target vehicle has a MOVED marker this turn and the attacker's line of fire falls in the target's Side arc, read the Gunnery Table one range band longer than the actual range (e.g., a shot at 750m against a crossing target uses the 1000m row instead).

**18.1a.7** Follow-up shot: if the firing unit's previous fire action this turn targeted the same vehicle and the firing unit has not moved since, read the Gunnery Table one range band shorter than the actual range (minimum: the shortest printed band). If both 18.1a.6 and 18.1a.7 apply to the same shot, apply the crossing adjustment first, then the follow-up adjustment, to the resulting band.

**18.1a.8** A Gunnery Roll is not required for anti-infantry fire (Rule 18.8), infantry anti-tank weapons (Rule 18.9), or Overrun (Rule 18.11) — those retain their existing resolution procedures unchanged.

**18.1a.9** Rule 6.6.7's existing −2 rFP penalty for opportunity fire against a moving target does not stack with Rule 18.1a.6 when the target is a vehicle — the Gunnery Roll's own crossing-target adjustment replaces it for vehicle targets specifically. Rule 6.6.7 continues to apply exactly as written when the target is infantry.

*NOTE: The Gunnery Roll reuses the exact 1d6+1d8+1d12 combination already rolled for every other attack in the game (Rule 8.5.1) rather than introducing new dice. Its two thresholds are derived from that combination's own exact probability distribution (Appendix C) so that "roll ≥ X" always means precisely what the printed thresholds say, at the resolution the combination allows — 24 distinct probability steps from about 0.17% to 100%. Below that floor, a shot is an automatic miss rather than requiring an unrollable threshold; this matters at the long ranges where a raw hit-probability calculation would otherwise round to a number smaller than any single die combination can represent.*

*NOTE: The crossing-target and follow-up-shot adjustments (18.1a.6–18.1a.7) trade some numerical precision for reusing the same printed table under all circumstances — no second table, no arithmetic, no die-roll modifier to remember. A more precise version would recompute exact thresholds for the crossing and follow-up cases directly; this was not done, since the "shift one band" approximation keeps the whole Gunnery Roll to a single printed reference and a single comparison, which was judged more valuable than the marginal accuracy gain. This is this document's own estimate of the trade-off, not something validated by playtesting — worth revisiting if a specific gun/range combination turns out to misbehave under it.*

## 15. Shatter gap (optional/advanced rule module)

A real, counter-intuitive phenomenon (Bird & Livingston Ch.7, p.29-33): a kinetic round that badly over-penetrates a plate — not marginally, but by a comfortable margin — can shatter its own nose against the armor before it finishes punching through, and fail. This is the historical explanation for otherwise-puzzling combat reports (76mm APCBC "penetrating" Tiger's driver plate at 1125m on paper, but Normandy veterans reporting it only worked inside 50 yards) and is a source of real satisfaction for players who know the history — but it is also actively confusing to a newer player ("why didn't my clearly-superior shot penetrate?"), which is why it's scoped as an **optional/advanced module**, not a change to the core 18.2 resolution table.

**Decisions locked this session** (via direct question to Rod, not inferred):
- **Resolution is deterministic, not a new roll.** If a shot's Shatter Gap check triggers, the result is simply forced to Non-Penetrating Hit (18.4) — no new dice, no modifier to remember on an existing roll. This matches the source's own confident framing (it presents the 1.06-1.22 ratio band as a real physical threshold, not a probability distribution) and keeps the addition as cheap as any single-comparison rule already in the game.
- **Module scope: optional/advanced, not core.** Off by default; a scenario or campaign can turn it on. Keeps base-game resolution untouched for newer players per the original three-persona review's concern, while giving veteran/advanced tables the deeper simulation they're likely to want.

**The physics, and why it doesn't fit the existing 18.2 band directly.** 18.2's existing bands (§ Rule 18.2 above) are flat, absolute-mm margins: PEN ≥ AV+3 is automatic, PEN < AV−3 is a bounce. Shatter gap is fundamentally a *ratio* (penetration/resistance, not penetration-minus-resistance) — the source's own recommended thresholds are 1.05 (upper edge of normal), 1.06-1.22 (shatter-gap failure), and beyond 1.22 ("shatter penetration": the round still gets through despite shattering). A ratio-based effect can't be folded into a fixed ±3mm band without either being wrong at one end of the game's AV range or requiring the player to multiply at the table — so this is built as its own small, separate check instead, following the same "universal reference table, not per-vehicle arithmetic" pattern already used for HEAT.

**Eligibility — ammo family.** Applies to *all* steel kinetic nose shapes: `capped` (APCBC/APC), `apbc` (Soviet-style blunt nose), and `ap_uncapped` (solid shot) — the source explicitly states shatter gap "would be expected to occur with Russian, British, Italian and American steel projectiles" (p.30), not just APCBC. It does **not** apply to tungsten/APDS rounds: Appendix 9 (p.104) directly shows APDS test rounds failing in a pattern that does *not* follow this ratio ("may be due to excessive angle between nose and flight path... possibility also exists that APDS shatter fails... which have not been determined") — a real but different, unsourced failure mode, deliberately not modelled here rather than misapplied. HEAT has no kinetic nose at all and needs no explicit exclusion — it isn't a family this mechanism can even be asked about.

**Eligibility — caliber vs. armor (approximating the source's T/D ≥ 0.8 condition).** Shatter gap only occurs when the plate is thick relative to the attacking projectile's diameter — a small/medium gun trying to punch through disproportionately thick armor, not a big gun overmatching thin plate. Rather than compute T/D at the table, this is expressed as a direct comparison of two numbers already printed on the counters in play: **the attacking gun's caliber (mm) must not exceed the target's AV.** This is a deliberately conservative rounding of the source's actual T/D ≥ 0.8 threshold up to T/D ≥ 1.0 (caliber ≤ raw thickness would be T/D ≥ 1.0 exactly; using AV instead of raw thickness is itself already a simplification, since AV already includes slope/cast/hardness corrections) — it will occasionally exclude a real historical shatter-gap case that the stricter 0.8 threshold would have caught, but never wrongly includes one, and needs zero arithmetic.

**Eligibility — impact velocity ≥ 2000fps.** Not independently checked. A shot only gets deep enough into over-penetration to land in the shatter window at the higher-velocity, closer-range part of a gun's curve — so this condition is treated as usually implied by the PEN-in-window check itself, not verified per gun. Flagged as a simplifying assumption, not confirmed true for every gun in the roster.

**Validation.** `shatter_gap_window()` reproduces both of the chapter's own worked examples: Tiger E driver plate (100mm@10°, AV=103mm) → window (109.2, 125.7)mm, source states "109mm to 126mm"; Panther mantlet (100mm cast @10°, AV=98mm) → upper bound 119.6mm, source states "120mm". It also correctly classifies a real Appendix 9 firing-test row not used to derive the formula at all: 17pdr APCBC vs. Tiger 82mm/50° side armor (AV=82mm), 3035fps shot, 87mm penetration (ratio 1.061, just inside the window) → actual historical test result "No Pen.", matching the shatter-gap prediction. Of the six APCBC rows in that same Appendix 9 table, this is the only one where PEN comfortably clears AV+3 (i.e., where 18.2's existing table would have called it automatic penetration) yet the real test failed — precisely the case this mechanism exists to catch, and the other five rows (all either clearly under AV or within a percent of it) needed no shatter-gap explanation and produce none.

**Implemented and tested** (`formulas.py`: `shatter_gap_window`, `shatter_gap_failure`, `shatter_gap_reference_table`; `pipeline.py`: `write_shatter_gap_reference_csv` → `shatter_gap_reference_output.csv`, an 18-row AV-banded table from 30mm to 200mm in 10mm steps), 8 new regression tests, all passing.

### Proposed rule text (Advanced Rule 18.2a)

**18.2a Shatter Gap (Optional Rule).** *If this module is in use for the scenario:*

**18.2a.1** Before applying Rule 18.2, check Shatter Gap if all of the following hold: (a) the firing ammunition is Capped, Uncapped AP, or Soviet APBC nature (not Tungsten/APDS, not HEAT); (b) the attacking gun's printed caliber (mm) does not exceed the target's AV in the profile/arc being hit.

**18.2a.2** If 18.2a.1's conditions hold, look up the target's AV on the Shatter Gap Table (player aid card) to find its Shatter Window — a lower and upper PEN value.

**18.2a.3** If effective PEN falls within the Shatter Window (inclusive), the shot is forced to a Non-Penetrating Hit (Rule 18.4), regardless of what Rule 18.2 would otherwise indicate. Proceed directly to 18.4 — do not consult 18.2 or 18.3 for this shot.

**18.2a.4** If effective PEN falls outside the Shatter Window (either below it, or above it), resolve normally via Rule 18.2 — Shatter Gap does not apply.

*NOTE: This can only ever make an otherwise-favorable shot worse, never better — it has no effect on shots where PEN < AV already. A round already headed for Rule 18.4 or a bounce is unaffected by this check either way.*

**Open items this rule text does not resolve:** whether nose-hardness variation by specific gun/round (the source notes 90mm APCBC "may not be vulnerable to shatter gap failure... nose damage appears minimal at all impact velocities," implying not every capped round is equally susceptible) should exempt specific guns once that data exists for this roster's own guns — not modelled, since no per-gun nose-hardness data exists for any gun currently in the 13-vehicle roster; whether the T/D ≥ 0.8-vs-1.0 rounding (see above) is worth tightening with a second, more precise threshold for advanced players who want it; whether this module should also apply to infantry AT weapons' kinetic rounds (PIAT's spigot mortar bomb is HEAT and already excluded, but Panzerfaust/Bazooka are also HEAT — in practice no infantry AT weapon in this game currently fires a kinetic round, so this open item may be moot until one is added).

## 16. HEAT vs. Schürzen (spaced standoff armor)

**Provenance flag, upfront:** neither of this project's two sources — Bird & Livingston, or John D. Salt's compilation — discusses HEAT's interaction with spaced/skirt armor anywhere. This was confirmed directly this session, not assumed: a full search of Bird & Livingston's Ch.9 ("Spaced Plates, Layered Armor and Edge Effects," the chapter that would obviously cover it) turned up only kinetic-round spaced-armor equations (the Okun formula) and a note about a *kinetic* AP round's HE-filled *burster charge* detonating early against spaced plate — a completely different phenomenon from a HEAT shaped-charge jet. Ch.17 ("Tactics to Maximize Armor Protection," p.83) was also checked in full and covers only oblique-approach geometry against kinetic rounds and turret-cutback silhouette reduction. An earlier note in this document (§3.3) had incorrectly assumed Ch.9 covered this — that was a mistaken inference, now corrected. **Everything below is general, well-established shaped-charge engineering knowledge and a wargame-design convention, not a citation to either project source** — flagged at lower confidence than the rest of this document accordingly.

**The physics (medium confidence, general knowledge).** A HEAT round's shaped-charge liner collapses on detonation into a high-velocity metal jet that needs to travel a specific "standoff" distance to fully form before it reaches peak penetration — too little standoff and the jet hasn't finished forming; too much and it begins to disperse into discrete, less-effective particles. Most WW2-era HEAT munitions (including infantry weapons like the Panzerfaust and Bazooka) were fuzed — often via a nose standoff probe or spike — to detonate close to their own optimal distance already. Schürzen (thin steel skirt plates, typically 5-30mm, mounted roughly 30cm from the main hull/turret side) forces the jet to begin forming at the skirt rather than at the round's own intended point, so by the time it reaches the real armor it has already traveled well past its optimal coherence distance and lost significant effectiveness. This is why Schürzen was historically a meaningful defense against HEAT specifically, while being essentially irrelevant (Ch.9's sourced finding: sometimes even a slight net negative) against kinetic AP/APCBC/tungsten rounds — two unrelated defeat mechanisms, addressed by the same piece of hung sheet metal for entirely different physical reasons.

**The number: 50% HEAT PEN reduction.** This specific figure is **not** a measured result from either project source — it matches a long-standing wargame-design convention (e.g. Advanced Squad Leader's own "Side Skirts" vehicle note halves HEAT/HEAT-based-weapon effects against the protected arc), chosen deliberately since Rod's stated goal is an ASL-like system and this is a figure ASL veterans will already recognize. It is grounded in real (if variable) physics rather than picked arbitrarily, but should be understood as a design convention, not a citation-backed constant the way `slope_multiplier` or `high_hardness_multiplier` are.

**Scope decisions:**
- **HEAT-only.** Schürzen gives no benefit against kinetic rounds in this system, consistent with Ch.9's own sourced finding for thin spaced plates generally. Worth stating explicitly in the rule text since "more armor is always better" is an intuitive but wrong assumption for this specific case.
- **Side arc only.** The historical norm — hull and/or turret side skirts — not front or rear, which is where the mass of Schürzen photographic and combat-report evidence points. A vehicle with genuine rear-arc standoff protection would need its own note; none in the current roster do.
- **Applies to infantry HEAT weapons too** (Panzerfaust, Bazooka, PIAT) — the mechanism is about the target's protection, not the size of the attacking round, so the rule is written once and referenced by every HEAT-capable weapon rather than duplicated per weapon type.
- **Which vehicles get it, this pass:** Panzer IV Ausf H (Hull Side, Turret Side — both hull skirts and turret "Thoma shields" were near-standard production features by this vehicle's modeled period), StuG III Ausf G (Hull Side — commonly photographed fitted from 1943 production), and **Panzer III Ausf M (Hull Side, added in the follow-up pass)** — schürzen became a standard running-production feature on the PzIII/PzIV line from roughly March 1943, within this vehicle's own 1943 era. **Deliberately not added to Panther Ausf G**: turret-side Schürzen existed on Panther but was a less universal, more variably-fitted feature than on Panzer IV — flagged as an open item below rather than guessed at with a blanket flag. **Deliberately not added to Tiger I Ausf E** either, on a firmer basis than "unknown": Tiger's turret-side "Thoma shields" were a field/factory feature from roughly spring 1944 onward — *after* this vehicle's modeled 1943 era, not merely undocumented.
- None of the schürzen vehicle-fitment facts above come from either project source (both were searched directly this session — "Thoma," "side skirt," "hull skirt," "turret skirt" all return zero hits) — general historical knowledge, flagged accordingly, same confidence tier as the 50% multiplier itself.

**Implemented and tested** (`formulas.py`: `heat_pen_vs_schurzen`; `data/vehicles.csv`: `schurzen` boolean column, set for the four arc rows named above; `pipeline.py`: `write_roster_csv` now passes the flag through to `roster_output.csv`), 1 new regression test, all passing (69 total).

### Proposed rule text (addendum to the HEAT rules, wherever Rule 18.2's HEAT handling lives)

**18.2b Schürzen (Standoff Skirt Armor).** A vehicle counter with a Schürzen marker/icon on a given Hull or Turret Side arc has standoff skirt armor protecting that arc against HEAT specifically.

**18.2b.1** Before comparing effective PEN to AV (Rule 18.2), if the attack (a) uses HEAT ammunition of any kind — gun-fired HEAT round, Panzerfaust, Bazooka, PIAT — and (b) targets a Side arc bearing a Schürzen marker, halve the attacking weapon's effective PEN (round down) before proceeding.

**18.2b.2** Schürzen has no effect on Capped, Uncapped AP, Soviet APBC, or Tungsten/APDS attacks. Do not apply any modifier for those ammunition natures, regardless of arc.

**18.2b.3** Schürzen has no effect on attacks against any arc other than the marked Side arc — Front and Rear (Hull or Turret) resolve HEAT attacks normally even on a vehicle with a Schürzen marker elsewhere on its counter.

*NOTE: This rule is not built from this project's two primary ballistics sources — neither covers HEAT-vs-standoff-armor interaction. The 50% figure follows established wargame-design convention (recognizable to ASL players specifically) rather than a specific measured test result; treat it as a reasonable, historically-grounded approximation rather than a precise figure the way this document's sourced formulas are.*

**Open items this rule text does not resolve:** whether Panther Ausf G's real but less-universal turret-side Schürzen fitment should get its own flag, a "sometimes" note, or a scenario-specific special rule rather than being omitted entirely; whether Schürzen's secondary historically-documented use against Soviet 14.5mm anti-tank rifles (PTRD/PTRS) is worth modelling — not in scope this pass, since the game does not currently model AT rifles as a distinct weapon type with their own PEN value; whether any other roster vehicle (or a future addition) should carry the flag once its own historical fitment is checked.

## 17. Face-hardened armor (Ch.3)

Picked as the concrete way to make progress on the "Western hardness deviation" open item (§7): rather than continue looking for a general BHN dataset for German/American/British armor that this project's sources simply don't provide, this tackles a *specific*, well-sourced mechanism the same two sources do cover in real depth — face-hardening, which was actual, deliberate German policy on exactly two vehicles already in this roster (Panzer III, Panzer IV).

**The physics.** A thin (2.5-5mm) surface layer hardened to 450-650 BHN over an otherwise homogeneous (220-300 BHN) core. Its whole point is defeating early-war ammunition that lacked an armor-piercing cap: the hardened face shatters the projectile's unprotected nose before it digs in. Once capped ammunition (APC/APCBC) became standard — British APC in early 1943, American 75mm/37mm APCBC already in 1942 — the cap protects the nose from that exact shattering mechanism, and the advantage **reverses**: face-hardened plate performs *worse* than homogeneous plate of the same thickness against capped rounds specifically. Slope-multiplier *shape* stays similar to homogeneous either way (Ch.3's own 12-case comparison, average error 3.7%) — only the base 0°-equivalent multiplier differs, which is what this adds.

**The numbers, triangulated against two of the chapter's own worked examples, not assumed:**
- **Capped rounds: 0.80** (a 20% penalty). Source gives only range figures, not a direct multiplier — "PzKpfw IVH driver plate (85mm@10°) vs. 75mm APCBC: if homogeneous, would limit penetration to 150m; face-hardened version penetrated at 940m instead." Triangulated using this project's own fitted Sherman 75mm M61 APC gun curve: PEN@150m = 86.65mm, matching the homogeneous effective-0°-resistance figure (86.35mm) to <0.4% — an independent cross-check confirming the gun curve is self-consistent with this worked example *before* trusting it to derive the face-hardened figure. PEN@940m = 68.18mm; dividing by the raw 85mm thickness gives 0.80.
- **Uncapped AP: 1.303** (a 30% bonus). Directly stated, no triangulation needed: "2pdr AP could penetrate 86mm of homogeneous armor at 0 yards/0° impact, but limited to 66mm of face-hardened penetration at same range/angle" — 86/66 = 1.303. One data point, an early-war low-velocity gun.
- **APDS: ~0.84** (a 16% penalty). Directly stated as a vulnerability ratio: "For 17 pdr APDS, face-hardened armor appears to be more vulnerable than homogeneous armor by factors of 1.155 to 1.229" — resistance multiplier is the reciprocal; midpoint used (1/1.192 = 0.839).
- **US tungsten (hvap76/hvap90): borrows the APDS figure** as the nearest analog — no sourced figure exists for these specifically, same "borrow the closest curve" pattern used elsewhere in this system, flagged rather than left unhandled.
- **Soviet APBC: no correction (1.0)** — no sourced figure at all for this ammo type against face-hardened plate. A real gap, not a claim of parity.
- **HEAT: not applicable, structurally.** Face-hardening's mechanism is specifically about shattering a kinetic round's nose — a shaped-charge jet has no equivalent failure mode. `heat_effective_resistance()` never touches `ArmorPlate`, so this can't leak in by accident.

**Scope decision — which plates get the flag, and why not more:**
- **Panzer IV Ausf H, Hull Front and Hull Side: yes.** The Front plate *is* the source's own worked example (PzKpfw IVH driver plate) — a direct, clean match. Side is covered by the explicit 1942 policy ("all German tanks to carry face-hardened armor on hull front/side plates in 30-50mm range") and PzIV's 30mm side sits right in that bracket.
- **Panzer IV Ausf H, Hull Rear and both Turret arcs: no.** The 1942 policy is stated as "front/side" only — rear is a real, deliberate scope boundary, not an oversight. No source discusses PzIV turret face-hardening at all.
- **Panzer III Ausf M: still deliberately excluded, but with a sharper reason now.** Follow-up research directly confirmed (Ch.3 p.22) that "Germans retained face-hardening late-war (Panther, PzKpfw IVF/G/H, PzKpfw III, StuG III)" — Panzer III as a *family* is explicitly named, stronger evidence than before. But this roster's specific plate is modelled as a 50mm base + 20mm applique composite (one flat 70mm slab in the data), and the base 30-50mm bracket almost certainly covers the 50mm portion while a bolt-on applique typically wasn't itself re-hardened. Applying the flag to the full 70mm would discount the whole plate by 20% when only ~71% of it is actually confirmed face-hardened — a real overcorrection, not a conservative choice. The blocker is now precisely identified (this system doesn't model the plate as two explicit layers, unlike the Ch.9 spaced/layered-armor cases elsewhere) rather than vaguely "no data."
- **StuG III Ausf G, Hull Side: yes, added in the follow-up pass.** Ch.3 p.22 directly names StuG III among the vehicles retaining face-hardened armor late-war, and its 30mm side plate sits squarely within the confirmed 30-50mm policy bracket.
- **StuG III Ausf G, Hull Front: still not flagged.** Same family citation applies, but the only vehicles the source names for "thicker plates" beyond the base bracket are Panther and PzKpfw IVH specifically — StuG III's 80mm glacis isn't named there, and isn't backed by a direct worked example the way PzIV's 85mm driver plate is. A thickness-bracket mismatch, not a coin flip.

**Live roster impact, checked directly:** Panzer IV Ausf H's Hull Front AV-vs-Capped drops from 81.2mm to 64.9mm, Hull Side from 29.7mm to 23.8mm. StuG III Ausf G's Hull Side drops from 29.7mm to 23.8mm (identical treatment to Panzer IV's side, as expected — same thickness, same multiplier). AV-vs-HEAT is confirmed unchanged for all affected plates.

Implemented and tested (`formulas.py`: `face_hardened_multiplier`, `ArmorPlate.face_hardened` field; `data/vehicles.csv`: `face_hardened` column, set for the three rows named above; `pipeline.py`: threaded through `resolve_av`/`resolve_av_tungsten` and passed to `roster_output.csv` as an informational column), 6 new regression tests, all passing (69 total).

~~**Open items this addition does not resolve:** whether Panzer III's applique-armor composite should get some partial/reduced version of this correction once modelled as two explicit layers rather than one flat slab~~ — **resolved, see §20.** ~~Whether StuG III Ausf G's Hull Front should eventually get the flag too~~ — **decided: no, and treated as a final call, not left open indefinitely.** Also considered and rejected: modelling it as a layered 50mm-base+30mm-applique composite the way Panzer III's was resolved (§20) — no source confirms StuG III Ausf G's specific glacis was actually built that way (vs. a single thicker plate), and assuming the same construction without evidence would be inventing data, not resolving a gap. Stays unflagged until a source specifically addresses this vehicle's own glacis construction. Remaining open item: the broader "Western hardness deviation" item (§7) is *narrowed*, not closed — this addresses face-hardening specifically, but German/American/British homogeneous armor's plain BHN (as opposed to face-hardened surface treatment) is still unchecked (confirmed searched, not found) against the ~230-240 BHN baseline the slope formulas assume.

## 18. Turret AV area-weighting for the rest of the roster

Extends the §7 open item: Tiger, Panther, and Sherman M4A1 already had real, vehicle-specific area-weighted turret figures (Ch.10's edge-effect data, a session-built Panther-specific weighting, and Ch.8's M34A1 hit-distribution table respectively). Everything else in the roster still assumed a rounded turret front behaves like a flat plate at one fixed angle — a real simplification, not a deliberate choice, just unfinished work until now.

**The tool that closes most of the gap: Ch.11's own general-purpose distribution.** Re-reading Ch.11 ("Turret Hit Probability and Rounded Mantlets," p.43-45) more carefully surfaced something not fully used before: the chapter gives *two* separate impact-angle distributions, not one. Panther's own turret figure already uses the first — a 5-band distribution the source explicitly ties to "hits at 200m range strike the Panther mantlet," a specific 76mm APCBC/200m analysis. The *second* is a finer 16-band distribution the source itself frames as general-purpose: "based on analysis of impact angles on a rounded mantlet when the hits are evenly distributed, which might occur at medium to long range or against hull down vehicles... converted to a 1-100 decimal dice system for use in war games." That's a direct invitation to reuse it for any rounded mantlet, not just Panther's.

**The mechanism.** `rounded_mantlet_angle_distribution()` returns this general 16-band table (angle → weight, derived directly from the source's own dice-score ranges, e.g. "70°: dice 2-4" → 3 of 100). `area_weighted_av()` takes a plate template and this distribution, samples `ArmorPlate.av_vs()` at each listed angle (holding thickness, cast, BHN, flaw, and face-hardening fixed — only the vertical angle varies), and returns the probability-weighted average. This is a genuine architectural improvement over how Tiger/Panther/Sherman's *hardcoded* `av_override_mm` figures work today: because it calls `av_vs()` internally rather than embedding a precomputed number, every other correction (cast deficiency, the 375 BHN gate, flaws, face-hardening) chains through automatically and stays live if the underlying data changes — no manual re-derivation needed the way the three existing overrides currently require.

**A real, substantial finding, not a modest tweak — and worth understanding why.** Applying this to six vehicles produced increases of roughly 40-80%, not a few percent:

| Vehicle | Turret Front AV-vs-Capped, before → after |
|---|---|
| Panzer IV Ausf H | 50 → 79 |
| T-34 Model 1943 | 43 → 56 |
| T-34/85 | 87 → 146 |
| KV-1S | 76 → 129 |
| T-70 | 24 → 31 |
| Sherman M4A3 (76mm) | 57 → 94 |

This size of jump is real, not a bug — sanity-checked directly: applying this same general table to Panther's own 100mm apex thickness (ignoring Panther's real edge-taper, purely as a same-methodology comparison) gives 168mm, the right ballpark below Panther's actual 249mm figure (which also benefits from real edge-thickening the general table can't see). The mechanism: the slope multiplier for capped rounds grows steeply and convexly with angle (a plate at 70-75° can present 5-8× its 0° resistance) — so even though the *bulk* of the distribution's weight sits at low angles (40% at 0-20°), the minority of hits landing at 50-75° (24% of the distribution) contributes disproportionately to the weighted average, because their individual resistance values are so much larger. Breaking down T-34/85's own 146mm figure: roughly a third of it (46mm) comes from the 0-20° band, and the remaining two-thirds accumulates steadily across every band from 25° to 75° — not a fluke driven by one extreme outlier, but the genuine shape of a probability-weighted average over a convex function. This also has a real geometric intuition behind it, independent of the formula: a domed/curved surface presents more of its visible silhouette at oblique local angles than a flat plate does (the same reason a sphere's visible area is dominated by its edge, not its center) — so a meaningful fraction of "hits that land somewhere on a rounded mantlet" genuinely do strike at a steep local angle even when the aim point itself is centered.

**Which vehicles got it, and which deliberately didn't:**
- **Rounded/curved, cast, and applied:** Panzer IV Ausf H (rounded "Topfblende" pot mantlet, standard from mid-1943), T-34 Model 1943, T-34/85, KV-1S (all classic domed cast Soviet turrets), T-70 (small cast/welded turret), Sherman M4A3 (76mm) (T23 turret, cast rounded gun shield).
- **Deliberately excluded — Panzer III Ausf M.** Its late-war gun mantlet was boxy/faceted, not a deeply curved dome the way Panzer IV's or the Soviet turrets were — applying a rounded-mantlet distribution to a flat-faced mount would be a mismatch, not an improvement. Kept as a flat-plate assumption, a considered decision rather than an oversight.
- **AV-vs-HEAT is unaffected for all six**, matching the same precedent already set by Tiger/Panther/Sherman M4A1: HEAT keeps using the plate's single thickness/angle rather than an area-weighted figure. Confirmed directly in the pipeline output (e.g. Panzer IV Turret Front AV-vs-HEAT stayed at 50.8mm through this change).
- **Only Turret Front got this treatment**, not Side/Rear. The source's distribution specifically describes hits landing across a mantlet *as viewed from roughly the front* — it says nothing about how hits distribute across a turret's side profile, which is a different shape/exposure question this source doesn't address.

**Implemented and tested** (`formulas.py`: `rounded_mantlet_angle_distribution`, `area_weighted_av`; `data/vehicles.csv`: new `rounded_mantlet` column, set for the six turret-front rows named above; `pipeline.py`: `resolve_av`/`resolve_av_tungsten` route through `area_weighted_av()` when the flag is set), 5 new regression tests, all passing (69 total).

**Open items this addition does not resolve:** whether the 5° angle spacing in Ch.11's dice table is fine enough, or whether a smoother interpolated distribution would materially change the result — not checked, since the source only gives discrete 5° bands to begin with; whether Sherman M4A1's turret (already using the real, vehicle-specific M34A1 table) should be cross-checked against what this general method alone would have predicted, as a further validation point — not done this pass; whether StuG III Ausf G's casemate front (also a curved cast superstructure, at least partially) should get any area-weighting treatment — casemates present a fundamentally different hit-probability picture (no separate turret to traverse, whole superstructure is "front") that this chapter's mantlet-specific framing doesn't obviously extend to, left alone rather than guessed at.

## 20. Panzer III's applique armor, modelled properly

Closes an item flagged twice already (§17, §19): whether Panzer III Ausf M's 50mm base + 20mm bolted applique hull front could get face-hardening applied without over-discounting the non-face-hardened applique portion. Resolving it needed a formula this project had read but never implemented — Ch.9's layered-plates-in-contact equation.

**The formula, validated properly before use.** Ch.9 p.38: two homogeneous plates bolted/welded together resist *less* than a single plate of the same combined thickness (a real, counter-intuitive finding from real test data — U.S. Navy deck-armor tests and a Sherman-applique field comparison). `layered_plate_effective_thickness(first_hit_mm, underlying_mm)` implements the book's own regression-fit equation, `0.3129 × (plate_ratio)^0.02527 × (max_thickness)^0.2439`, applied to the combined thickness with the source's own stated floor/ceiling. Checked against all three of the chapter's own worked examples before trusting it with Panzer III's numbers: Sherman 38mm+38mm → 57.7mm (book: 58mm), T-34 15mm-scrap-over-45mm → 49.5mm floor-limited (book: ~50mm), KV-1 30mm-over-75mm → 92.0mm (book: 92mm) — all within rounding.

**Applied to Panzer III's hull front**: 20mm applique (hit first) + 50mm base plate → 56.0mm effective, not the naive 70mm sum this roster had been using. That alone is a real, sourced correction independent of face-hardening.

**Partial face-hardening — this project's own construction, clearly labelled as such.** Only the 50mm base plate is confirmed face-hardened (Ch.3's 30-50mm policy bracket); the bolt-on applique almost certainly isn't (typically simple homogeneous stock). Neither project source describes how to treat a partially face-hardened composite plate — there's no equation to look up. Added `ArmorPlate.face_hardened_fraction` (0.0-1.0), which linearly blends `face_hardened_multiplier()` with 1.0 (no correction) by that fraction, used instead of the existing all-or-nothing `face_hardened` boolean. Set to 50/70 ≈ 0.714 for this plate — the base plate's share of the raw combined thickness. This is an engineering judgement call, not a sourced value, and documented as one directly in `vehicles.csv`'s notes field, not left implicit.

**A real, substantial result, checked directly**: Hull Front AV-vs-Capped drops from 75.8mm (flat 70mm, no corrections) to 51.4mm — a ~32% reduction, roughly 60% of it from the layering discount and the rest from the partial face-hardening penalty. AV-vs-Tungsten also correctly picks up the layered 56mm figure (57.0mm) since tungsten is also a kinetic penetrator the Ch.9 mechanism applies to.

**A second-order bug caught in the same pass, before it shipped**: the naive implementation would have also fed the layered 56mm thickness into the HEAT calculation (`resolve_av_heat()`'s fallback to `thickness_mm` when `heat_thickness_mm` is unset). HEAT's jet-penetration mechanism has no relationship to the kinetic plugging-shear failure Ch.9's discount is derived from — there is no sourced basis for assuming it carries over. Fixed by setting explicit `heat_thickness_mm=70`/`heat_vertical_deg=21` overrides for this plate, so HEAT sees the true raw combined thickness, undiscounted. Confirmed directly in the pipeline output (AV-vs-HEAT: 75.0mm, matching the raw 70mm@21° figure, not the kinetic-adjusted 56mm).

Implemented and tested (`formulas.py`: `layered_plate_effective_thickness`, `ArmorPlate.face_hardened_fraction`; `data/vehicles.csv`: new `face_hardened_fraction` column, `heat_thickness_mm`/`heat_vertical_deg` override added for this plate; `pipeline.py`: threaded through `resolve_av`/`resolve_av_tungsten`), 8 new regression tests, all passing (80 total).

**Open items this addition does not resolve:** whether 50/70 is the *right* blend fraction, or whether face-hardening's benefit/penalty doesn't scale linearly with material fraction at all (unknown — no source addresses this); whether the applique is even bolted in *front of* the base plate (assumed, based on typical historical practice) rather than behind it, which would change which thickness is "first hit" in the layered-plate formula.

## 22. Two remaining Gunnery Roll design decisions, closed

Both had been sitting as "not decided" since §14 first drafted the Gunnery Roll rule text.

**Infantry AT weapons (Panzerfaust/Bazooka/PIAT): decided, no Gunnery Roll.** Three independent reasons converged rather than one deciding factor:
1. **These weapons are already deliberately built around a range constraint, not an accuracy constraint.** Appendix E.42 (written well before this session) already frames Panzerfaust's flat, always-penetrating PEN as a considered choice: the tactical tension is "close to suicidal range or don't get a kill," not "aim well or miss." A hit/miss roll on top would dilute the exact mechanic the weapon is designed around.
2. **No sourced calibration data exists for this.** The vehicle Gunnery Roll's thresholds are fitted to real data (Appendix 17's British O.B. Investigation No.659 study, 15 real calibration points). Nothing comparable exists in either project source for infantry-fired AT weapons — a Gunnery Roll here would be invented, not sourced, breaking with how every other addition this session has been built.
3. **These are already single, climactic actions.** An exposed AT gunner takes one shot at extreme personal risk; a second roll layered on top adds bookkeeping without a clear payoff for what that moment represents.

**Overrun's point-blank fire: decided, and turned out simpler than the original framing implied — it was never really an independent fork.** Pre-entry defensive fire (Rule 18.11.2) *is* infantry firing AT weapons at a vehicle — Rule 18.9's exact domain, which the decision above already resolves. The vehicle's simultaneous MG fire at the infantry was already outside the Gunnery Roll's scope for an unrelated, pre-existing reason (18.1a.8 exempts all anti-infantry fire, Rule 18.8). There is no point anywhere in Overrun's resolution where a vehicle fires at *another vehicle* — so the Gunnery Roll was never actually applicable to any part of it, and "should this route through the Gunnery Table" was answered by the infantry-AT-weapon decision, not a separate one.

Implemented in the rulebook directly (`section_18.rst` 18.9 and 18.11 intros rewritten from "open item, not decided" to stated decisions with reasoning) — no code changes, this was a rules-design question, not a physics one.
